Introduction

Motivation

As robots increasingly enter human environments — from healthcare to education to social assistance — the ability to perceive and respond to human emotions becomes critical for natural interaction. Traditional robotic arms operate as blind actuators with no awareness of the humans around them. This project bridges that gap by creating an emotion-aware robotic arm that perceives, interprets, and physically reacts to human facial expressions in real-time.

System Overview

The system is a 3-DOF serial manipulator (RRR configuration) driven by an end-to-end AI pipeline. A webcam captures the scene, YOLOv8 detects and localizes faces, DeepFace recognizes the dominant emotion, and a 3-layer noise-resilient filter stabilizes the output before mapping it to physical servo poses on the Arduino-controlled arm.

AI Pipeline

The emotion recognition pipeline processes each frame through four stages:

1
Face Detection — YOLOv8n-face provides bounding box coordinates with GPU acceleration for real-time performance.
2
Emotion Recognition — DeepFace analyzes the cropped face region and returns probability scores across 7 base emotions.
3
3-Layer Filtering — Confidence gating, derived emotion logic (EXCITED, BORED), and majority-vote stabilization eliminate noise and prevent jitter.
4
Servo Control — TrackerController blends face tracking with emotion poses: Base tracks horizontally (gain 0.25, 8px deadzone), shoulder blends 80% emotion + 20% vertical tracking, elbow is purely emotion-driven. EMA smoothing (α=0.30) prevents jitter. Rate-limited to ~12 commands/sec.
5
OLED Feedback — The Arduino receives emotion labels via EMO: commands and renders expressive pixel faces (eyes, mouth, eyebrows) on a 128×64 SSD1306 OLED display, giving the robot a visible emotional expression.
02

Novelty

01

3-Layer Noise-Resilient Pipeline

Raw emotion predictions from deep learning models are inherently noisy — flickering between states multiple times per second. Our system implements a unique 3-layer stabilization approach:

  • EmotionFilter — Window-based confidence gating with dominance margin (rejects ambiguous predictions)
  • DerivedEmotionEngine — Temporal compound logic for detecting EXCITED and BORED states
  • EmotionStateMachine — Majority-vote sliding window ensures only persistent emotions trigger motion
02

Compound Derived Emotions

Beyond the 7 base emotions from DeepFace (happy, sad, angry, surprise, fear, disgust, neutral), we derive two additional states using temporal compound logic:

  • EXCITED — Triggered when happy > 0.6 AND surprise > 0.2 simultaneously
  • BORED — Triggered when neutral persists > 0.9 confidence for over 5 seconds

These derived emotions create richer, more nuanced physical expressions.

03

Aggressive Face Tracking

The arm doesn't just recognize emotions — it physically follows the user's face in real-time. A proportional controller computes base and shoulder angles from face position error:

  • Base (horizontal) — Gain: 0.25 · error + emotion offset, range 60°–120°
  • Shoulder (vertical) — 80% emotion pose + 20% tracking (gain 0.25), range 0°–60°
  • Elbow — 100% emotion-driven, range 30°–150°
  • Deadzone — 8px threshold, EMA α=0.30, ≥2° change gate

The arm appears "obsessed" with following the user, reacting instantly to head movements.

04

OLED Emotion Face Display

The robot physically wears its emotion. A 128×64 SSD1306 OLED display mounted on the arm renders pixel-art faces corresponding to each detected emotion:

  • HAPPY — Round eyes + upward smile curve
  • SAD — Round eyes + downward frown curve
  • ANGRY — Eyes with slanted angry eyebrows + flat mouth
  • SURPRISE — Wide eyes + open O-shaped mouth
  • BORED — Half-line eyes + flat mouth
  • NEUTRAL — Normal eyes + straight line mouth

Methodology

Robot Configuration

The manipulator is a 3-DOF RRR (Revolute-Revolute-Revolute) serial manipulator. Joint 1 (Base) rotates about the vertical axis, while Joints 2 (Shoulder) and 3 (Elbow) rotate in the vertical plane, forming a classic anthropomorphic arm configuration.

Base Height ($d_1$) 5.0 cm
Upper Arm ($a_2$) 9.0 cm
Forearm ($a_3$) 8.0 cm

Denavit-Hartenberg Parameters

Using the standard Denavit-Hartenberg convention, each link is described by four parameters: joint angle $\theta_i$, link offset $d_i$, link length $a_i$, and link twist $\alpha_i$.

Link $i$ $\theta_i$ $d_i$ (cm) $a_i$ (cm) $\alpha_i$
1 $\theta_1^*$ $d_1 = 5.0$ $0$ $90°$
2 $\theta_2^*$ $0$ $a_2 = 9.0$ $0°$
3 $\theta_3^*$ $0$ $a_3 = 8.0$ $0°$

* denotes variable joint angle

Forward Kinematics

The general DH transformation matrix for each link $i$ is:

$$A_i = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta_i & -\sin\theta_i\cos\alpha_i & \sin\theta_i\sin\alpha_i & a_i\cos\theta_i \\ \sin\theta_i & \cos\theta_i\cos\alpha_i & -\cos\theta_i\sin\alpha_i & a_i\sin\theta_i \\ 0 & \sin\alpha_i & \cos\alpha_i & d_i \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Substituting the DH parameters for Link 1 ($\alpha_1 = 90°$, $a_1 = 0$, $d_1 = 5.0$) where $a_2 = 9.0$ cm and $a_3 = 8.0$ cm:

$$A_1 = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta_1 & 0 & \sin\theta_1 & 0 \\ \sin\theta_1 & 0 & -\cos\theta_1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & d_1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

For Link 2 ($\alpha_2 = 0°$, $a_2 = 9.0$, $d_2 = 0$):

$$A_2 = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta_2 & -\sin\theta_2 & 0 & a_2\cos\theta_2 \\ \sin\theta_2 & \cos\theta_2 & 0 & a_2\sin\theta_2 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

For Link 3 ($\alpha_3 = 0°$, $a_3 = 8.0$, $d_3 = 0$):

$$A_3 = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta_3 & -\sin\theta_3 & 0 & a_3\cos\theta_3 \\ \sin\theta_3 & \cos\theta_3 & 0 & a_3\sin\theta_3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

The overall transformation from the base frame to the end-effector is $T_0^3 = A_1 \cdot A_2 \cdot A_3$. The end-effector position is extracted from the last column:

$$\boxed{p_x = \cos\theta_1 \left( a_2\cos\theta_2 + a_3\cos(\theta_2 + \theta_3) \right)}$$ $$\boxed{p_y = \sin\theta_1 \left( a_2\cos\theta_2 + a_3\cos(\theta_2 + \theta_3) \right)}$$ $$\boxed{p_z = d_1 + a_2\sin\theta_2 + a_3\sin(\theta_2 + \theta_3)}$$

Inverse Kinematics

Given a desired end-effector position $(p_x, p_y, p_z)$, the joint angles are computed analytically using geometric decomposition.

Step 1
Base angle from the XY projection: $$\theta_1 = \text{atan2}(p_y,\; p_x)$$
Step 2
Reduce to a 2-link planar problem: $$r = \sqrt{p_x^2 + p_y^2}, \qquad s = p_z - d_1$$

where $r$ is the radial distance in the XY plane and $s$ is the height above the shoulder.

Step 3
Elbow angle via the law of cosines: $$\cos\theta_3 = \frac{r^2 + s^2 - a_2^2 - a_3^2}{2\,a_2\,a_3} = D$$ $$\boxed{\theta_3 = \text{atan2}\!\left(\pm\sqrt{1 - D^2},\; D\right)}$$

The $\pm$ gives two solutions: elbow-up ($+$) and elbow-down ($-$).

Step 4
Shoulder angle: $$\boxed{\theta_2 = \text{atan2}(s,\; r) - \text{atan2}\!\left(a_3\sin\theta_3,\; a_2 + a_3\cos\theta_3\right)}$$

Workspace & Reachability

A target point is reachable if and only if $|D| \le 1$. The workspace of this 3-DOF arm is a hollow torus section defined by:

$$r_{\min} = |a_2 - a_3| = 1.0 \text{ cm}, \qquad r_{\max} = a_2 + a_3 = 17.0 \text{ cm}$$

Combined with the base rotation range and joint limits, this defines the physical workspace envelope of the manipulator.

Control Strategy

The Arduino firmware implements a smooth-motion-with-velocity-kick algorithm. Each servo moves toward its target with two speed zones:

  • Far zone (distance > 15°): Fast step of 4° per cycle — rapid response
  • Near zone (distance ≤ 15°): Slow step of 1° per cycle — precision settling

This dual-zone approach provides quick response without overshoot, with a 10 ms delay between steps ensuring smooth mechanical motion. All servo values are clamped to hardware-safe limits before writing.

Serial command format: B:90 S:20 E:120\n

Control Architecture

The arm uses a dual-loop always-tracking controller with emotion blending:

B
Base Servo — Face tracking (horizontal) + emotion offset. Gain: 0.25 · error, 8px deadzone. EMA smoothing α=0.30.
S
Shoulder Servo — 20% face tracking (vertical) + 80% emotion pose blend. Gain: 0.25 · error, EMA smoothing α=0.30.
E
Elbow Servo — 100% emotion-driven (pure gesture expression). Changes only when stable emotion switches.

Future Work

IK-Based Face Tracking

Replace the proportional face-tracking controller with a full inverse kinematics solver — map face pixel coordinates to 3D workspace points and solve for joint angles analytically.

Multi-Face Priority

Extend the system to detect and prioritize multiple faces simultaneously, tracking the closest or most emotionally active subject.

Trajectory Planning

Implement smooth trajectory generation (e.g., cubic spline interpolation in joint space) between emotion poses for more natural, fluid arm movements.

Servo Calibration UI

Build a graphical tool for calibrating servo angle limits and tuning emotion-to-pose mappings without modifying code.

Extended Emotion Set

Add more derived compound emotions (e.g., CONFUSED, AMUSED) and explore reinforcement learning for discovering novel arm expressions.

ROS 2 Integration

Port the system to ROS 2 for standardized message passing, enabling integration with other robotic platforms and simulation tools like Gazebo.
